1. System Parameters & Machine Constants

All signal models are grounded in the physical geometry of the Wilo pump-motor unit operating on a 50 Hz European power grid. The following constants are used throughout every fault generator.

1.1 Motor & Shaft Frequencies

Synchronous speed (8-pole, 50 Hz):
\[ n_{sync} = \frac{120 \cdot f_{line}}{p} = \frac{120 \times 50}{8} = 750 \text{ RPM} \]
Rated shaft frequency (with slip):
\[ f_{shaft} = \frac{n_{rated}}{60} = \frac{730}{60} \approx 12.17 \text{ Hz} \]
where \(p = 8\) poles, \(f_{line} = 50\) Hz, rated speed ≈ 730 RPM
Line harmonics (50 Hz system):
\[ f_{1\times} = 50 \text{ Hz},\quad f_{2\times} = 100 \text{ Hz},\quad f_{3\times} = 150 \text{ Hz},\quad f_{5\times} = 250 \text{ Hz} \]

1.2 Bearing Defect Frequencies

For a 6-ball bearing with pitch diameter \(D = 50\) mm, ball diameter \(d = 10\) mm, and contact angle \(\alpha = 15°\):

Ball Pass Frequency — Outer Race (BPFO):
\[ \text{BPFO} = \frac{N_b}{2} \cdot f_{shaft} \cdot \left(1 - \frac{d}{D}\cos\alpha\right) = \frac{6}{2} \times 12.17 \times \left(1 - \frac{10}{50}\cos 15°\right) \approx 29.4 \text{ Hz} \]
Ball Pass Frequency — Inner Race (BPFI):
\[ \text{BPFI} = \frac{N_b}{2} \cdot f_{shaft} \cdot \left(1 + \frac{d}{D}\cos\alpha\right) \approx 43.6 \text{ Hz} \]
Ball Spin Frequency (BSF):
\[ \text{BSF} = \frac{D}{2d} \cdot f_{shaft} \cdot \left[1 - \left(\frac{d}{D}\cos\alpha\right)^2\right] \approx 11.8 \text{ Hz} \]
\(N_b = 6\) balls, \(D = 50\) mm pitch diameter, \(d = 10\) mm ball diameter, \(\alpha = 15°\) contact angle

1.3 Pump Blade-Pass Frequency

Blade-Pass Frequency (BPF):
\[ \text{BPF} = N_{vanes} \times f_{shaft} = 5 \times 12.17 \approx 60.8 \text{ Hz} \]
5-vane centrifugal impeller; each vane generates one pressure pulse per revolution

3. Statistical Feature Extraction

The following 11 statistical features are computed for every sensor channel (acceleration, current, audio) at every recording interval. Let \(\mathbf{x} = [x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_N]\) be a cleaned signal vector (NaN and ±∞ removed).

3.1 Central Tendency

Mean:
\[ \mu = \bar{x} = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^{N} x_k \]
Median: \[ \tilde{x} = \text{middle value of the sorted sequence } x_{(1)} \leq x_{(2)} \leq \cdots \leq x_{(N)} \]

3.2 Dispersion

Standard Deviation:
\[ \sigma = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^{N} (x_k - \bar{x})^2} \]
Variance:
\[ \sigma^2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^{N} (x_k - \bar{x})^2 \]
Range:
\[ R = x_{max} - x_{min} = \max_k(x_k) - \min_k(x_k) \]

3.3 Energy & Amplitude Metrics

Root-Mean-Square (RMS):
\[ x_{RMS} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^{N} x_k^2} \]
RMS represents the effective energy content of the signal. For a pure sine wave, \(x_{RMS} = A/\sqrt{2}\) where \(A\) is the peak amplitude.

Peak Value:
\[ x_{peak} = \max_{k} |x_k| \]
Crest Factor:
\[ CF = \frac{x_{peak}}{x_{RMS}} \]
Crest factor measures the ratio of peak amplitude to RMS. A pure sine wave has CF = √2 ≈ 1.414. Early bearing faults increase CF significantly (CF > 5) because isolated impulses raise the peak without yet raising the RMS. At late-stage failure, as RMS grows rapidly, CF may fall back toward lower values.
Load Factor (operating point scalar):
\[ \lambda_{load} = \frac{\text{actual operating load}}{\text{rated full load}} \in [0.60,\; 1.10] \]
Stored as a metadata scalar, not computed from the signal. Randomised per run to ensure ML training coverage across operating conditions.

3.4 Shape Metrics (Higher-Order Statistics)

Skewness (Fisher's moment coefficient):
\[ \gamma_1 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^{N} \left(\frac{x_k - \bar{x}}{\sigma}\right)^3 \]
\(\gamma_1 = 0\) for a symmetric distribution (Gaussian, pure sine). Positive skewness → tail on the right (impulsive positive spikes). Negative skewness → tail on the left. Bearing faults and electrical arc transients drive |γ₁| upward.

Excess Kurtosis (Fisher's definition):
\[ \gamma_2 = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{k=1}^{N} \left(\frac{x_k - \bar{x}}{\sigma}\right)^4 - 3 \]
\(\gamma_2 = 0\) for a Gaussian signal (excess kurtosis = 0, absolute kurtosis = 3). Impulse events (bearing impacts, arc discharges) produce heavy tails → high kurtosis.
Bearing fault kurtosis profile:
Stage 1: \(\gamma_2 \approx 0\) (Gaussian noise only)
Stage 2: \(\gamma_2\) rises to 12–15 (isolated BPFO transients)
Stage 3: \(\gamma_2\) peaks ~25 then drops as damage spreads
Stage 4: \(\gamma_2\) = 3–5, but RMS grows 5–6× (widespread spall)

3.5 Complete Feature Vector

For each sensor \(s \in \{\text{acceleration, current, audio}\}\) and each mode \(m \in \{\text{max, min, combined}\}\), the stored feature vector is:

\[ \mathbf{f}_{s,m} = \left[\mu,\; \tilde{x},\; \sigma,\; \sigma^2,\; x_{min},\; x_{max},\; R,\; x_{RMS},\; x_{peak},\; \gamma_1,\; \gamma_2,\; CF,\; \lambda_{load}\right] \]
13 features × 3 sensors × 3 modes = up to 117 statistical features per interval, plus 5 top FFT peaks per sensor (15 frequency + 15 amplitude values).

4. Spectral Analysis — FFT Algorithm

4.1 Hann-Windowed FFT

Raw FFT without windowing suffers from spectral leakage — energy from a pure tone "bleeds" into neighbouring frequency bins, masking fault signatures. The Hann window eliminates this.

Step 1 — Mean removal (DC suppression):
\[ z_k = x_k - \bar{x} \]
Step 2 — Hann window:
\[ w_k = \frac{1}{2}\left[1 - \cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi k}{N-1}\right)\right], \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1 \] \[ \tilde{z}_k = z_k \cdot w_k \]
Step 3 — Real FFT (single-sided spectrum):
\[ \hat{Z}[n] = \sum_{k=0}^{N-1} \tilde{z}_k \cdot e^{-j2\pi kn/N}, \quad n = 0, 1, \ldots, \tfrac{N}{2} \]
Frequency axis:
\[ f_n = \frac{n \cdot f_s}{N}, \quad n = 0, 1, \ldots, \tfrac{N}{2} \]
Step 4 — Normalised single-sided amplitude:
\[ A[n] = \frac{2}{\sum_{k=0}^{N-1} w_k} \cdot |\hat{Z}[n]| \]
Factor of 2 accounts for the discarded negative-frequency half. Dividing by the window sum (not by N) ensures the amplitude of a tone is the same regardless of window shape. For \(N = 1400\) and Hann window: \(\sum w_k \approx 700\), so the normalisation factor is \(2/700 \approx 2.86 \times 10^{-3}\).

4.2 Peak Detection

Valid frequency range:
\[ \mathcal{F}_{valid} = \{f_n : f_n \geq 5.0 \text{ Hz}\} \]
Frequencies below 5 Hz are excluded to suppress DC bias bleed and sub-rotation thermal drift.

Minimum peak separation:
\[ \Delta f_{min} = 2.5 \text{ Hz} \quad \Rightarrow \quad d_{bins} = \left\lfloor \frac{\Delta f_{min}}{\Delta f} \right\rfloor = \left\lfloor \frac{2.5}{0.5} \right\rfloor = 5 \text{ bins} \]
Prevents adjacent-bin duplicates caused by smearing of a true single-frequency peak across 2–3 bins.

Top-5 peaks returned:
\[ \{(f^*_1, A^*_1),\; (f^*_2, A^*_2),\; \ldots,\; (f^*_5, A^*_5)\} \] where peaks are sorted by amplitude descending: \(A^*_1 \geq A^*_2 \geq \cdots \geq A^*_5\).

4.3 Full-Spectrum Downsampling

For frontend charts, the full spectrum (up to 350 bins above 5 Hz) is compressed to 500 output points using maximum-in-bucket aggregation:

\[ b = \frac{|\mathcal{F}_{valid}|}{500}, \quad (f_{out}^{(i)},\; A_{out}^{(i)}) = \arg\max_{f_n \in \text{bucket}_i}\; A[n] \]
Maximum-in-bucket preserves spectral peaks; a stride-based approach would randomly alias peaks away. This guarantees fault-frequency peaks are always visible in the frontend chart.

5. Impact Response Physics Models

Mechanical and electrical impulse events (bearing impacts, arc discharges, bubble collapses) excite structural resonances. The system response to a single impulse is modelled as a damped sinusoidal decay.

5.1 Single Damped Impact Response

Damped natural frequency:
\[ \omega_d = \omega_n \sqrt{1 - \zeta^2} \]
Response to a single impulse at time \(t_{imp}\):
\[ x(t) = \begin{cases} A \cdot e^{-\zeta \omega_n (t - t_{imp})} \cdot \sin\!\bigl[\omega_d (t - t_{imp})\bigr] & t \geq t_{imp} \\ 0 & t < t_{imp} \end{cases} \]
\(A\) = impact amplitude (units of signal, e.g. m/s²),\quad \(\omega_n\) = undamped natural frequency (rad/s),\quad \(\zeta\) = damping ratio (\(0 < \zeta < 1\)).
For the motor steel frame: \(\omega_n = 2\pi \times 1500\) rad/s, \(\zeta = 0.08\).

5.2 Periodic Impact Train

Bearing defects and blade-pass events produce a periodic train of impacts. Real machinery exhibits log-normally distributed impact amplitudes and small timing jitter:

Impact train signal:
\[ x_{train}(t) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} A_k \cdot e^{-\zeta\omega_n(t - t_k)} \sin[\omega_d(t - t_k)] \cdot \mathbf{1}(t \geq t_k) \]
Impact amplitudes (log-normal):
\[ A_k \sim \text{LogNormal}(\ln \bar{A},\; \sigma_A^2) \quad \Rightarrow \quad A_k > 0 \text{ always} \]
Log-normal is used because impact energy is always positive and naturally right-skewed — consistent with real bearing data.

Impact timing with jitter:
\[ t_k = k \cdot T_{impact} + \epsilon_k, \quad \epsilon_k \sim \mathcal{U}(-\eta \cdot T_{impact},\; +\eta \cdot T_{impact}) \]
\(T_{impact} = 1/f_{impact}\) is the nominal period (e.g. \(1/\text{BPFO} \approx 34\) ms).
\(\eta = 0.05\) = jitter fraction (5% of period) simulates realistic shaft speed fluctuation.

Stage Boundaries

Let \(\phi = n / n^*\) (normalised interval fraction, \(0 \leq \phi \leq 1\)): \[ \text{Stage} = \begin{cases} 1 & 0 \leq \phi \leq 0.25 \\ 2 & 0.25 < \phi \leq 0.55 \\ 3 & 0.55 < \phi \leq 0.82 \\ 4 & 0.82 < \phi \leq 1.0 \;\text{(system failure at } \phi = 1) \end{cases} \]
Severity index (0 → 1 across Stages 2–4):
\[ s = \text{clip}\!\left(\frac{\phi - 0.25}{0.75},\; 0,\; 1\right) \]

Acceleration Signal Model

Baseline (all stages):
\[ x_{base}(t) = a_{nom}\lambda_{load}(1 + 0.15s) + 0.15\,a_{nom}\lambda_{load}\sin(2\pi f_{shaft} t) + \varepsilon_b(t) \] \[ \varepsilon_b \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\; [0.04\,a_{nom}\lambda_{load} + 0.02s]^2) \]
Stage 2 — BPFO outer-race impacts:
\[ x_{accel}(t) = x_{base}(t) + x_{train}(t;\; \text{BPFO},\; A_{BPFO}) + M_{shaft}(t)\cdot x_{train}(t;\; \text{BPFI},\; A_{BPFI}) \] \[ A_{BPFO} = (0.3 + 1.5\phi_2)\lambda_{load}, \quad A_{BPFI} = 0.15\phi_2\lambda_{load} \] where \(\phi_2 = ({\phi - 0.25})/({0.30})\) is Stage-2 local progress, and \[ M_{shaft}(t) = 0.5 + 0.5\left|\sin(\pi f_{shaft} t)\right| \] is the shaft-rate amplitude modulation of the inner-race impact train.

Stage 3 — Peak then declining amplitude:
\[ A_{BPFO} = (2.5 - 0.8\phi_3)\lambda_{load}, \quad A_{BPFI} = (2.0 - 0.6\phi_3)\lambda_{load} \] \[ \varepsilon_{floor} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\; [0.3 + 1.2\phi_3]^2) \quad \text{(rising noise floor)} \]
Stage 4 (system failure) — Widespread spall:
\[ x_{accel}(t) = x_{train}(t;\;\text{BPFO},\; 5.5a_{nom}\lambda_{load}\cdot 0.6,\; \sigma_A = 0.6) + x_{train}(t;\;\text{BPFI},\; 5.5a_{nom}\lambda_{load}\cdot 0.5,\; \sigma_A = 0.6) + \mathcal{N}(0,\; [5.5a_{nom}\lambda_{load}\cdot 0.35]^2) \]

Current Signal Model (Bearing)

Bearing friction increases mechanical slip → slight current rise.

Pre-failure (gradual): \[ i(t) = I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}(1 + 0.05s) + \mathcal{N}(0,\; [I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}\cdot 0.02]^2) \] Stage 4 (seized bearing — locked rotor attempt): \[ i(t) = I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}\cdot 4.5 + \mathcal{N}(0,\; 2.0^2), \quad i(t) \in [40,\; 120] \text{ A} \]

6.2 Motor Electrical Fault — Phase Imbalance

Phase imbalance and line-to-ground faults create slip sidebands and a growing 2× line harmonic in vibration. Arc transients are modelled as damped sinusoidal impulses.

Slip frequency:
\[ s_{pct} \sim \mathcal{U}(0.02,\; 0.06) \quad (2\text{–}6\% \text{ slip}) \] \[ f_{slip} = s_{pct} \times f_{line} \approx 1\text{–}3 \text{ Hz} \]
Slip sideband frequencies:
\[ f_{lower} = f_{line} - 2f_{slip}, \qquad f_{upper} = f_{line} + 2f_{slip} \]

Acceleration Model (Electrical Fault)

Severity index:\( \quad s = \text{clip}(n/n^*,\; 0,\; 1) \)

Magnetic pull at 2× line — growing 100 Hz vibration:
\[ x_{em}(t) = (0.08 + 1.8s)\sin(2\pi f_{2\times}t + \varphi_0) \] Arc transients (probability per sample):
\[ P_{arc} = 0.001 + 0.05s \] \[ x_{arc}(t) = \sum_{k:\; u_k < P_{arc}} A_k^{arc} \cdot e^{-\zeta\omega_n(t-t_k)}\sin[\omega_d(t-t_k)], \quad A_k^{arc} \sim \text{LogNormal}(\ln(0.4 + 1.5s),\; 0.35^2) \]

Current Model (Phase Imbalance)

\[ i(t) = I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}(1 + 0.12s) + A_{sb}\sin(2\pi f_{lower}t + \varphi_0) + A_{sb}\sin(2\pi f_{upper}t) + A_{h3}\sin(2\pi f_{3\times}t + \varphi_0) + i_{arc}(t) + \varepsilon(t) \] \[ A_{sb} = 0.04\,s\,I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}, \qquad A_{h3} = 0.05\,s\,I_{FLA}\lambda_{load} \]
Slip sidebands (\(f_{line} \pm 2f_{slip}\)) are the primary electrical signature. The 3rd harmonic (150 Hz) grows from phase imbalance stress.

6.3 Motor Overheating — Exponential Thermal Model

Temperature rise follows Newton's Law of Cooling (first-order system response to a step heat input).

Winding temperature rise above ambient:
\[ \Delta T(n) = \Delta T_{ss} \left(1 - e^{-n/\tau_{th}}\right) \]
\(\Delta T_{ss} \sim \mathcal{U}(80°C,\; 130°C)\) — steady-state temperature rise at saturation.
\(\tau_{th} = 0.4\,n^*\) — thermal time constant in intervals (40% of run duration).
\(n\) = current interval number, \(n^* \in [11, 15]\) = critical interval.

Thermal severity (normalised):
\[ s_{th} = \text{clip}\!\left(\frac{\Delta T(n)}{\Delta T_{ss}},\; 0,\; 1\right) = \text{clip}\!\left(1 - e^{-n/\tau_{th}},\; 0,\; 1\right) \]
Winding resistance with temperature:
\[ R_{winding}(T) = R_0 \left(1 + \alpha_{Cu}\,\Delta T\right), \qquad \alpha_{Cu} = 0.00393\;°C^{-1} \]
As winding temperature rises, resistance increases. To maintain torque at constant load, slip increases, causing a slight (≤10%) current rise.

Acceleration Model (Overheating)

Thermal expansion → 1× shaft amplitude growth: \[ x_{1\times}(t) = a_{nom}\lambda_{load}(0.3 + 0.8\,s_{th})\sin(2\pi f_{shaft}t) \] Thermal cycling ripple (≈1.5 Hz): \[ x_{ripple}(t) = 0.5\,s_{th}\sin(2\pi \times 1.5\; t) \]

Current Model (Overheating)

Slip-induced DC rise + 3rd harmonic growth: \[ i(t) = I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}(1 + 0.05\,s_{th}) + 0.06\,s_{th}\,I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}\sin(2\pi f_{3\times}t) + 0.03\,s_{th}\,I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}\sin(2\pi \times 0.8\;t) + \varepsilon(t) \]
3rd harmonic (150 Hz) grows with insulation stress. The 0.8 Hz term represents very-slow thermal cycling modulation of the load current.

6.4 Motor Shaft Misalignment

Shaft misalignment has two physically distinct components: angular (dominates 1× RPM) and parallel (dominates 2× RPM). Each run randomly selects the mix:

\[ \lambda_{ang} \sim \mathcal{U}(0.3,\; 0.7) \quad \text{(angular fraction)} \] \[ A_{1\times} = \lambda_{ang}(0.2 + 2.8\,s)\lambda_{load} \] \[ A_{2\times} = (1 - \lambda_{ang})(0.15 + 3.2\,s)\lambda_{load} \]
Acceleration signal:
\[ x_{accel}(t) = a_{nom}\lambda_{load} + A_{1\times}\sin(2\pi f_{shaft}t + \varphi_1) + A_{2\times}\sin(2\pi \cdot 2f_{shaft}\cdot t + \varphi_2) + \varepsilon(t) \]
Phase angles \(\varphi_1, \varphi_2 \sim \mathcal{U}(0, 2\pi)\) are fixed per run — real misalignment has a fixed but unknown phase relative to any measurement trigger.
Kurtosis stays near 1.5 (pure sinusoids, not impulsive) — unlike bearing faults.

Current — torque modulation at 2× shaft:
\[ i(t) = I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}(1 + 0.25\,s) + 0.15\,s\,I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}\sin(2\pi \cdot 2f_{shaft}\cdot t + \varphi_2) + \varepsilon(t) \]

6.5 Motor Stall — Locked-Rotor Event

Motor stall is a sudden fault. Pre-fault baseline is flat (healthy). At the stall interval, the rotor decelerates to zero.

Rotor deceleration model (chirped shaft frequency):
\[ f_{shaft}(t) = f_{shaft,0}\cdot e^{-t/\tau_{decel}}, \qquad \tau_{decel} = 0.3 \text{ s} \]
Vibration during stall transition:
\[ x_{decel}(t) = a_{nom}\lambda_{load}\,e^{-t/\tau_{decel}} \cdot \sin\!\left(2\pi \int_0^t f_{shaft}(\tau)\,d\tau\right) \] \[ + \underbrace{0.08(1 - e^{-t/\tau_{decel}})\sin(2\pi f_{2\times}t)}_{\text{EM hum building as rotation stops}} \]
The chirped sinusoid decays to zero (rotor stops). Simultaneously, the electromagnetic hum at 100 Hz builds as the rotor locks in the AC flux field.

Locked-rotor current (LRC):
\[ i_{LRC,peak} = I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}\cdot C_{LRC}, \qquad C_{LRC} \sim \mathcal{U}(6.0,\; 8.0) \] \[ i(t)\big|_{stall} = i_{LRC,peak}\cdot e^{-0.5\,t} + \mathcal{N}(0,\; 3^2) \]
The LRC decays slightly as rotor heats up (resistance increases, reducing current). Typical LRC = 6–8× full-load current for induction motors.

6.7 Motor Winding Failure — Insulation Breakdown

Turn-to-turn short circuits develop progressively. Odd current harmonics grow quadratically with the fraction of shorted turns. Partial discharge (PD) events are temporally clustered — one discharge lowers the threshold for the next (Weibull/self-triggering model).

Insulation health index:
\[ H(n) = \max\!\left(1 - \frac{n}{n^*},\; 0\right), \qquad s = 1 - H(n) \]
3rd harmonic growth (quadratic with shorted turns):
\[ A_{h3} = I_{FLA}\lambda_{load} \times 0.08\,s^2 \] 5th harmonic (≈60% of 3rd):
\[ A_{h5} = I_{FLA}\lambda_{load} \times 0.05\,s^2 \] Current signal:
\[ i(t) = I_{FLA}\lambda_{load} + A_{h3}\sin(2\pi f_{3\times}t + \varphi_3) + A_{h5}\sin(2\pi f_{5\times}t + \varphi_5) + i_{PD}(t) + \varepsilon(t) \]
The quadratic \(s^2\) growth reflects the physical relationship where harmonic current grows proportionally to (shorted turns)² due to the mutual inductance coupling between healthy and shorted turns.
Partial Discharge (PD) — self-triggering cluster model:
\[ P_{PD} = 0.0003 + 0.008\,s \quad (\text{per sample}) \] After each PD event at sample \(i\):
\[ \text{Follow-up events: } n_{cluster} \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_{cl}), \quad \lambda_{cl} = 2.5 \] at samples \(i + j\) where \(j \sim \mathcal{U}\{1, 14\}\), with a refractory period \(\sim \mathcal{U}\{5, 39\}\) samples before the next primary event.

6.8 Pump Cavitation — Rayleigh Bubble Collapse

Cavitation occurs when local static pressure drops below the vapour pressure \(p_v\) of the fluid. Vapour bubbles form and collapse violently (Rayleigh collapse) when they reach higher-pressure zones near the impeller vanes.

Rayleigh collapse time (spherical bubble):
\[ T_{collapse} = 0.915\,R_0\sqrt{\frac{\rho}{p_\infty - p_v}} \]
\(R_0\) = initial bubble radius, \(\rho\) = fluid density, \(p_\infty\) = far-field pressure.
Each collapse creates a pressure micro-jet exciting the pump casing resonance at \(\omega_{n,pump} = 2\pi \times 2000\) rad/s, \(\zeta_{pump} = 0.05\).

Collapse events cluster at BPF (pressure recovery repeats at each vane pass):
\[ x_{accel}(t) = x_{train}(t;\;\text{BPF},\; 3.5\lambda_{load},\; \omega_{n,pump},\; \zeta_{pump}) + x_{train}(t;\;2\cdot\text{BPF},\; 1.8\lambda_{load}) + x_{train}(t;\;0.5\cdot\text{BPF},\; 1.2\lambda_{load},\;\eta=0.12) \]
The 0.5×BPF sub-harmonic appears during developed cavitation from the recirculation zone at the impeller inlet — a well-documented cavitation diagnostic marker.
Current — pump efficiency loss:
\[ i(t)\big|_{cavitation} = 1.8\,I_{FLA}\lambda_{load} + 0.25\,I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}\sin(2\pi\,\text{BPF}\cdot t) + \varepsilon(t) \]
Cavitation reduces pump head → motor runs against recirculation loss → current rises (opposite of seal leakage). BPF ripple appears in current from periodic hydraulic impulse loading.

6.9 Pump Impeller Damage — Hydraulic Imbalance

A missing or deformed vane breaks the 5-fold symmetry of the impeller, creating hydraulic unbalance at 1× shaft and amplitude-modulated BPF vibration with sidebands.

BPF sidebands from amplitude modulation (AM):
\[ \text{Lower sideband: } f_{BPF} - f_{shaft} \approx 48.6 \text{ Hz} \] \[ \text{Upper sideband: } f_{BPF} + f_{shaft} \approx 73.0 \text{ Hz} \]
Amplitude-modulated BPF (shaft-rate envelope):
\[ x_{BPF,mod}(t) = x_{train}(t;\;\text{BPF},\;\ldots) \cdot \underbrace{\left(1 + 0.8\left|\sin(\pi f_{shaft}t)\right|\right)}_{E_{shaft}(t)} \]
Full post-damage signal:
\[ x_{accel}(t) = a_{nom}\lambda_{load} + 2.0\lambda_{load}\sin(2\pi f_{shaft}t + \varphi_0) + x_{BPF,mod}(t)\cdot E_{shaft}(t) + 0.8\lambda_{load}\sin(2\pi(f_{BPF} - f_{shaft})t) + 0.8\lambda_{load}\sin(2\pi(f_{BPF} + f_{shaft})t) + \varepsilon(t) \]

6.10 Pump Seal Leakage — Turbulent Leak Model

Mechanical seal failure allows fluid to escape around the shaft. The distinguishing signature is broadband turbulent hissing (300–3000 Hz), not the periodic BPF popping of cavitation.

Turbulent hiss (broadband Gaussian, amplitude-modulated by leak fraction):
\[ x_{audio}(t)\big|_{leak} = L_{base} + 8 + \mathcal{N}(0,\; [12\lambda_{leak}]^2) + x_{suction}(t) \] \[ \lambda_{leak} \sim \mathcal{U}(0.3,\; 1.0) \quad \text{(fraction of seal circumference failed)} \]
Current — running light (opposite of cavitation):
\[ i(t)\big|_{leak} = 0.75\,I_{FLA}\lambda_{load} + 0.4\lambda_{leak}\,I_{FLA}\lambda_{load}\sin(2\pi \times 0.5\;t) + \varepsilon(t) \]
When fluid escapes, the pump loses hydraulic load → motor draws less current. The 0.5 Hz oscillation models slow pressure-wave hunting as the pump cycles between loaded and unloaded states. This current-drop signature distinguishes seal leakage from all other faults.

Onset transient (seal rupture pressure wave):
\[ x_{transient}(t) = 2.5\lambda_{load}\,e^{-t/0.15}\sin(2\pi f_{shaft}t) \]

7. Deviation Scoring & Fault Severity

7.1 Baseline Estimation

The baseline (healthy reference) is computed from the first \(N_{baseline}\) intervals using a robust percentile window to reduce sensitivity to outliers:

Robust baseline for parameter \(\theta\):
\[ \mu_\theta^{baseline} = \text{median}\!\left\{\theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots, \theta_{N_{baseline}}\right\} \] \[ \sigma_\theta^{baseline} = \text{MAD} = \text{median}\!\left\{|\theta_k - \mu_\theta^{baseline}|\right\} \times 1.4826 \]
MAD (Median Absolute Deviation) scaled by 1.4826 is equivalent to the Gaussian standard deviation but robust to outliers. Primary sensor parameters get weight 1.0; secondary parameters get 0.4.

7.2 Z-score Normalisation

\[ z_\theta(n) = \frac{\theta(n) - \mu_\theta^{baseline}}{\sigma_\theta^{baseline}} \]
Stored alongside raw values. A z-score of 3 means the parameter is 3 standard deviations above the healthy baseline — typically indicating a significant deviation.

7.3 Weighted Deviation Score

Per-parameter deviation (direction-aware):
\[ d_\theta(n) = \begin{cases} \max(z_\theta(n),\; 0) & \text{if direction = ``up''} \\ \max(-z_\theta(n),\; 0) & \text{if direction = ``down''} \\ |z_\theta(n)| & \text{if direction = ``abs''} \\ |z_\theta(n)| & \text{if direction = ``any''} \end{cases} \]
Composite deviation score for fault type \(F\):
\[ D_F(n) = \frac{\sum_{\theta \in \Theta_F} w_\theta \cdot d_\theta(n)}{\sum_{\theta \in \Theta_F} w_\theta} \]
\(\Theta_F\) = set of parameters with non-zero weight for fault \(F\). \(w_\theta \in [0, 1]\) = fault-specific parameter weight. \(D_F\) is a normalised weighted average of z-scores.

7.4 Monotonic Trend Slope

A linear (least-squares) slope is computed over the deviation window to characterise the rate of degradation:

Least-squares slope:
\[ \hat{\beta}_\theta = \frac{\sum_{k=1}^{M} (k - \bar{k})(\theta_k - \bar{\theta})}{\sum_{k=1}^{M} (k - \bar{k})^2} \]
Computed over the \(M\)-interval deviation window (pre-fault trend). Stored as a training feature. A positive slope indicates a rising trend (degradation); negative slope indicates decline (e.g., vibration drops in motor stall).
